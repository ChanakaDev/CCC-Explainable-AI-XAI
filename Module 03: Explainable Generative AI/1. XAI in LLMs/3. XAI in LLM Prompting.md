# XAI in LLM Prompting

## Quick review: what is prompting?

**Prompting** designs task-specific instructions in natural language to guide an LLM's output **without altering its parameters**. A prompt typically combines:

- **Instruction** — what the model should do.
- **Context** — background information.
- **User input** — the actual query.

Many prompting methodologies exist (Sahoo et al., 2024). For explainability we focus on two:

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **In-Context Learning (ICL)** | Show the model task **demonstrations** as part of the prompt |
| **Chain of Thought (CoT)** | Prompt the model to describe its reasoning **step by step** |

---

## Explainability of In-Context Learning

**Question:** *How does in-context learning influence model behavior?*

Recent research probes this with:

- Contrastive demonstrations
- Perturbations of input text
- Flipped labels
- Semantically unrelated labels

### Liu et al. (2023) — contrastive demonstrations + saliency maps

Uses the **SST-2** sentiment analysis benchmark as a baseline. Builds contrastive demonstrations three ways:

- **Label flipping** — invert the labels in the demonstrations.
- **Input perturbation** — modify the input text.
- **Complementary explanations** — add extra explanatory content.

Then analyzes saliency maps to see how each variant changes the model's behavior.

### Wei et al. (2023) — semantic priors vs. input-label mappings

Investigates how ICL is affected by:

- **ICL with flipped labels** — does the model trust the demonstration labels or its semantic prior?
- **ICL with semantically unrelated labels** — words remapped to unrelated words to strip away semantic meaningfulness.

This isolates whether ICL relies on semantic priors learned during pre-training, or on the input-label mappings in the prompt itself.

## Explainability of Chain of Thought

**Question:** *How does CoT prompting affect LLM behavior?*

Standard CoT prompt structure: **Question (Q) → Thought (T) → Answer (A)**.

Recent research probes this with:

- Saliency scores
- Perturbations
- Counterfactuals

### Wu et al. (2023) — saliency scores

Analyzes saliency scores of input tokens — i.e., how influential each token is on the model's output. The goal: determine whether CoT prompting **changes saliency** compared to standard prompting, and explain *why* CoT improves performance.

### Two 2022 papers — perturbing CoT demonstrations

Add **errors** to CoT demonstrations in few-shot prompts to determine which aspects are essential for generating good explanations.

### Madonna et al. — counterfactual prompts

Proposes counterfactual prompts that perturb key components of a CoT prompt:

- Symbols
- Patterns
- Texts

## Uncertainty in LLMs

LLMs frequently exhibit **overconfidence**, returning high-certainty answers even when training data is incomplete or ambiguous. Risks include:

- Spreading misinformation.
- Driving critical decisions in healthcare, finance, etc., on unreliable outputs.

Quantifying uncertainty is therefore central to trustworthy AI.

### Consistency-based estimation — Xiong et al. (2024)

Generate **multiple responses** to the same question and use their **consistency** to estimate confidence. Two ways to introduce variation:

- **Self-consistency** — randomness in the answer-generation process.
- **Induced consistency** — adding misleading hints to the prompt.

Idea: a more consistent fine-tuned LM has higher estimated confidence.

### Token-level uncertainty — Duan et al.

LLMs generate text token-by-token. Token-level methods:

1. Compute a confidence score for each predicted token from its probability distribution.
2. Aggregate token-level uncertainties into an overall uncertainty estimate.

### Can we just ask the LLM how confident it is?

**Probably not.** Even research frameworks (Li et al., 2024) that have the LLM **justify** its confidence — rather than naively asking — face two fundamental issues:

1. **Hallucination.** LLMs don't know what they don't know, and will often invent a confidence-justifying narrative.
2. **No grounding.** It is difficult to map a self-reported confidence number to anything real.

> Self-reported confidence may be interesting, but it is **not** an explainable AI approach.
